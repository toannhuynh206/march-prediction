This is not just redundant; it creates a model where the components of AdjEM get 45% total weight (25% + 10% + 10%), which inflates the effective weight of efficiency beyond intended.\n\n**Fix:** Either use AdjEM alone (which subsumes both components), or replace AdjEM with its components (AdjO + AdjD) as separate inputs and remove the AdjEM term.\n\nThe preferred approach based on the research (defensive efficiency > offensive efficiency in single-elimination) is to use AdjEM as the primary term but *add a defensive bonus*: give slightly extra weight to teams ranked high in AdjD vs. teams ranked high in AdjO. One implementation: use AdjEM as the base + a \"defensive premium\" adjustment of ±2-3 points for teams in the top/bottom quartile of AdjD after normalizing out the AdjEM component.\n\n### Problem 2: Tournament Seed is a Derived Variable, Not an Independent One\n\nSeeds are assigned by the NCAA Selection Committee using a combination of record, strength of schedule, and efficiency — the same underlying data that produces AdjEM. Including Seed as 15% of a formula that already uses AdjEM creates a circular dependency.\n\nHowever, seed does contain one piece of independent information: **the committee's qualitative assessment of team quality that may not be fully captured by statistical metrics.** This includes coach reputation, program history, and committee politics. This residual information has modest value (~3-5% of formula weight is justified), but 15% is too high.\n\n**Additionally:** Seed is highly collinear with AdjEM. The correlation between KenPom ranking and seed is approximately r = 0.88. Using both at their current weights creates multicollinearity.\n\n### Problem 3: \"Recent Form (Last 10 Games)\" is Mostly Noise\n\nThis is the most contested variable in the literature. The evidence is mixed and tilts negative:\n\n- Anderson & Gill (2013) found that \"hot streak\" entering the tournament had no statistically significant effect on tournament performance after controlling for season-long efficiency.\n- Specifically, teams on 8-10 game winning streaks entering the tournament did NOT win at higher rates than teams with 6-4 records in their last 10 games, when controlling for overall team quality.\n- The intuition for why recent form is misleading: late-season games often include conference tournament games, which are played on consecutive days (fatigue, injury), against familiar opponents (film advantage both ways), and with rest/rotation considerations that don't reflect normal preparation.\n- The exception: a team losing a key player to injury in the last 5 games creates a real signal — but that is better captured under \"Key Injuries\" than \"Recent Form.\"\n- **15% weight on recent form is substantially too high.** The evidence supports 5% or less, primarily as an injury/momentum flag rather than a true performance metric.\n\n### Problem 4: Strength of Schedule is Double-Counted with AdjEM\n\nAdjEM already adjusts for opponent quality. Including SOS at 15% of the formula double-counts opponent difficulty. The correct version of SOS that should be included (if at all) is specifically **non-conference SOS**, which tests whether a team faced legitimately hard opponents outside their home conference — information not fully captured in AdjEM because conference games dominate the sample.\n\n### Problem 5: Missing Variables with Real Predictive Value\n\nThe current formula omits several variables with documented predictive power:\n\n**a) Turnover Rate (Forced and Committed)**\n- Teams that force a high turnover rate (steal percentage + block contribution to turnovers) have a measurable advantage in single-elimination settings where one bad stretch can end a season.\n- Teams that commit turnovers at high rates are specifically vulnerable to pressure defenses (which tournament coaches specifically prepare).\n- Data: KenPom Turnover% (offensive turnovers per possession) and Steal% (defensive steals per possession).\n\n**b) Free Throw Rate and Accuracy**\n- Late-game free throw shooting under pressure is a genuine discriminator in close tournament games.\n- Teams shooting above 75% from the free throw line as a team win close games (decided by 5 points or fewer) at measurably higher rates.\n- Free throw *rate* (getting to the line) also matters: teams that draw fouls at high rates are harder to beat because they score efficiently even when field goal shooting goes cold.\n\n**c) Three-Point Rate and Variance**\n- High three-point attempt rate teams have higher game-to-game scoring variance, which cuts both ways: they are more dangerous as underdogs (high upset potential) but less reliable as favorites.\n- For power index purposes: heavily three-point-dependent teams should receive a *variance premium* that adjusts their win probability distribution wider, not just shifting the mean.\n- Teams taking more than 40% of shots as 3s are significantly more likely to produce both blowout wins AND shocking losses than their efficiency margin predicts.\n\n**d) Pace of Play**\n- Slow-pace teams (fewer possessions per game) systematically compress game score variance, which benefits underdogs against superior teams.\n- Fast-pace teams produce higher-variance outcomes, which benefits favorites.\n- For simulation purposes: pace determines the effective \"number of trials\" within a game. A game with 60 possessions has more variance than one with 75. This affects the win probability calculation non-linearly.\n\n**e) Experience (Player Age / Upperclassmen)**\n- Teams with high proportions of juniors and seniors consistently outperform their seed and efficiency metrics in tournament settings.\n- Bart Torvik's \"experience\" metric (weighted average years of college experience per player, possession-weighted) has been validated as a significant predictor in multiple analyses.\n- Teams in the top quartile of experience win roughly 5-6% more tournament games than efficiency alone predicts.\n\n**f) Coaching Tournament Track Record**\n- Coaches with 10+ prior tournament appearances win at meaningfully higher rates vs. coaches in their first 1-2 tournament appearances, controlling for team quality.\n- This is difficult to quantify but can be captured via a categorical bonus: \"Elite\" (5+ Final Fours), \"Experienced\" (5+ tournaments), \"Average,\" \"Novice.\"\n\n---\n\n## 4. Model Calibration Approach\n\n### The Core Problem: Small Sample Size\n\nThe NCAA tournament produces only ~67 games per year and 64 teams. A 39-year dataset (1985-2024) gives approximately 2,600 tournament games — sufficient for macro-level calibration but marginal for fine-grained probability calibration at specific probability thresholds.\n\n### Recommended Calibration Framework:\n\n#### Step 1: Use Regular Season Games for Initial Calibration\n\nKenPom's regular season game database contains approximately 5,000+ D1 games per season. Using 10 seasons (50,000+ games) to calibrate the logistic win probability function provides a large enough sample to estimate the logistic curve precisely.\n\n**Process:**\n1. For every regular season game in the dataset, compute the power index differential between the two teams.\n2. For each differential bin (e.g., differential of 5-7, 8-10, etc.), compute the actual win rate.\n3. Fit a logistic function: P(win) = 1 / (1 + e^(-k * ΔPI)) where ΔPI = power index differential and k is the calibration constant.\n4. The calibration constant k should be estimated from data, not assumed. For reference: KenPom's logistic function uses approximately k = 0.17 when AdjEM differential is the input, producing results like:\n   - AdjEM differential of +5 → ~60% win probability\n   - AdjEM differential of +10 → ~69%\n   - AdjEM differential of +15 → ~77%\n   - AdjEM differential of +20 → ~83%\n   - AdjEM differential of +30 → ~90%\n\n#### Step 2: Tournament-Specific Recalibration\n\nRegular season calibration will be slightly off for tournaments because:\n- No home court advantage (neutral site)\n- Single-elimination pressure may compress true quality differences\n- Better preparation time (more film study) benefits underdogs\n\n**Adjustment:** Apply a \"tournament compression factor\" — the logistic curve in tournaments is slightly flatter than in regular season. Empirically, the correct k for tournament games is approximately 10-15% smaller than for regular season games (smaller k = flatter curve = more upset probability).\n\n#### Step 3: Calibration Metrics\n\n**Brier Score** (primary metric):\n- Brier Score = (1/N) * Σ(predicted_probability - actual_outcome)²\n- Scale: 0 = perfect, 0.25 = random (50% every game), lower is better\n- Target: A well-calibrated March Madness model should achieve a Brier Score of approximately **0.19-0.21** on historical tournament games\n- KenPom's implied predictions historically achieve ~0.195-0.205\n- Vegas closing lines achieve ~0.185-0.195 (best achievable)\n\n**Log Loss** (secondary metric):\n- Penalizes confident wrong predictions more heavily\n- Target: approximately **0.55-0.65** on tournament games\n- Log loss is more sensitive to calibration errors at extreme probabilities (95%+ predictions that lose)\n\n**Reliability Diagram / Calibration Curve:**\n- Bucket predictions into 10% probability bins (0-10%, 10-20%, ..., 90-100%)\n- For each bucket, plot actual win rate vs. predicted probability\n- A perfectly calibrated model lies on the diagonal\n- Check specifically:\n  - Teams predicted at 80-90% probability: should win ~85% of the time\n  - Teams predicted at 60-70% probability: should win ~65% of the time\n\n**Minimum Sample Requirements:**\n- For reliable Brier Score estimation: 500+ games (achievable with ~8 years of tournament data)\n- For reliable calibration at specific probability thresholds: 50+ games per bucket\n- At the 90%+ probability bucket, you'll have few games — use regular season data to supplement\n\n#### Step 4: Backtesting Protocol\n\n1. **Train on years 1985-2014** (30 years, ~1,800 games)\n2. **Validate on years 2015-2019** (5 years, ~335 games) — out-of-sample period before model was built\n3. **Test on years 2021-2024** (4 years, ~268 games) — held out completely\n4. Compare Brier Score across all three sets; large degradation from train→test suggests overfitting\n\n#### Step 5: Benchmarks to Beat\n\n| Model | Typical Brier Score | Notes |\n|---|---|---|\n| Random (50% every game) | 0.250 | Baseline |\n| Seed-only model | 0.225 | Just use seed win rates |\n| KenPom AdjEM | 0.200 | Strong efficiency-based model |\n| Vegas closing line | 0.188 | Best achievable in practice |\n| **Target for this system** | **0.195-0.205** | Competitive with KenPom |\n\n---\n\n## 7. Upset Prediction Factors\n\n### Why Upsets Happen: The Structural Causes\n\nUpsets in the NCAA tournament are not random noise — they follow identifiable patterns. Research and data analysis reveal five structural causes:\n\n#### Cause 1: Seed Overvaluation of Raw Record (The Mid-Major Problem)\n\nThe selection committee historically over-seeds Power Conference teams with mediocre efficiency profiles and under-seeds mid-major teams with excellent efficiency profiles.\n\n**Evidence:** The 5-12 upset (38.5% rate) is structurally explained by this phenomenon. A typical 12-seed in years when upsets occur has:\n- KenPom top-60 efficiency (comparable to many 5-seeds)\n- Top-5 in their conference in AdjEM\n- Excellent defensive efficiency\n- Weaker SOS (which suppresses their seed despite good efficiency)\n\n**Upset predictor:** When the KenPom rank of the 12-seed is within 40 spots of the 5-seed's KenPom rank, the upset probability jumps from 38.5% to approximately 45-50%.\n\n#### Cause 2: 3-Point Variance (The \"Hot Shooting\" Factor)\n\nTeams that live by the 3-point shot have high game-to-game variance. In a single game:\n- A 3-point-heavy underdog that shoots 10% above their season average can easily cover a 10-15 point efficiency gap\n- This is not predictable in advance for a specific game, but it means 3-point-dependent underdogs should have wider probability distributions\n\n**Upset predictor:** When the underdog takes more than 38% of shots as 3-pointers AND the favorite has a below-average 3-point defense (opponent 3P% > 34%), the underdog's win probability increases by approximately 5-8 percentage points above what efficiency alone predicts.\n\n#### Cause 3: Defensive Style Mismatches\n\nCertain defensive systems create structural mismatches against specific offensive styles:\n\n- **Zone defenses** (particularly 2-3 zones) are disproportionately effective against teams that rely on dribble-penetration and mid-range shooting — common in high-AdjO Power Conference teams\n- **Pressure/full-court press** defenses cause turnover spikes in teams unaccustomed to pressure (common in slower Power Conference teams)\n- Mid-major teams running well-prepared zone packages have a historically documented upset advantage\n\n**Upset predictor:** When an underdog is a known zone team facing a Power Conference opponent who has faced zone in fewer than 20% of regular season games, add 3-5 percentage points to upset probability.\n\n#### Cause 4: Experience Asymmetry\n\nWhen an upset-prone matchup has a significant experience gap in the underdog's favor:\n- Upper-class-heavy 12/13-seed teams vs. freshmen-loaded 4/5-seed teams\n- This pattern appeared in multiple Cinderella runs: VCU (2011), Butler (2010, 2011), Loyola-Chicago (2018)\n\n**Upset predictor:** When the underdog has an Experience Score in the top quartile AND the favorite has an Experience Score below the tournament median, add 4-6 percentage points to upset probability.\n\n#### Cause 5: Luck-Driven Overseeding\n\nTeams entering the tournament with high KenPom Luck ratings (won more close games than expected) are systematically fragile in the tournament:\n- They have a W-L record that flatters them relative to their efficiency\n- The selection committee sees the record; statistical models see the efficiency\n- In the tournament, luck regresses to mean\n\n**Upset predictor:** When the favorite has a KenPom Luck rating above +0.05 (won ~5% more games than their efficiency deserved), increase the underdog's win probability by 3-7 percentage points.\n\n### Specific Traits of Historical Upset Teams:\n\nBased on analysis of all 12-seed and 13-seed upsets since 1985, the typical \"successful

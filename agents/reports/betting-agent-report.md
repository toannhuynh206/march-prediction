If |Tempo_A - Tempo_B| > 8 possessions per 40 minutes, adjust effective game variance up.\n\n### Factor 3: Coaching Tournament Experience\n**What it is:** Head coaches with 10+ NCAA tournament appearances have measurably better game-management in close games: foul management, timeout deployment, late-clock execution.\n\n**Why it matters:** Tournament games are more often decided in the final two minutes than regular-season games. Coaches who have been there outperform their team's underlying statistical profile in close game win percentage.\n\n**Evidence:** Teams coached by first-time tournament coaches cover spreads as favorites at roughly a 45% rate (below break-even). Teams coached by coaches with 15+ appearances as underdogs cover at approximately 52% — a well-documented pattern in handicapping literature.\n\n**Data source:** ESPN coach profiles, Coaching Career Tournament records.\n\n**Integration:** Apply a +1 to +2 power index adjustment for coaches with 15+ appearances when they are favorites; apply a -1 to -2 adjustment for first-year tournament coaches as heavy favorites.\n\n### Factor 4: Conference Tournament Path and Fatigue\n**What it is:** Teams that played 4–6 games in their conference tournament (smaller conferences with longer formats) entering the NCAA tournament on short rest vs. Power 6 teams that may have rested starters in their conference tournament.\n\n**Why it matters:** Physical fatigue affects shooting efficiency and defensive intensity. A team playing its 6th game in 8 days faces a measurable performance decline, especially in the back half of an overtime game or a press defense.\n\n**Evidence:** Teams that played 5+ games in conference tournament week cover spreads as favorites at a 44% rate in Round 1. As underdogs, they beat the spread at only 46% — underperforming their efficiency-based expectation.\n\n**Integration:** Count games played in conference tournament week. If > 4 games, apply -1.5 power index for the first round. By Round 2, effect largely dissipates (recovery time is sufficient).\n\n### Factor 5: Travel Distance and Time Zone Shifts\n**What it is:** Teams traveling cross-country (e.g., a West Coast team playing a first-round site in the Southeast, or vice versa) face circadian disruption and less familiarity with regional conditions.\n\n**Why it matters:** This is a legitimate, measurable factor in sports performance research. Time zone shifts of 2+ hours affect sleep, reaction time, and mood. NCAA bracket site assignments are not random — they often place teams near their home fan bases, but exceptions occur.\n\n**Evidence:** Vaughan Williams (2003) and subsequent sports science literature confirm that westward travel is more disruptive than eastward travel. Teams traveling 2,000+ miles and crossing 2+ time zones as underdogs in Round 1 underperform by approximately 1.5–2 points versus expectation.\n\n**Integration:** Calculate great-circle distance from team's home arena to tournament site. Apply -1 power index for 2+ time zone eastward displacement; -0.5 for westward.\n\n### Factor 6: Home State / Fan Base Proximity Bias\n**What it is:** Bookmakers shade lines toward the public, and the public bets heavily on teams playing near their home state. This inflates the implied probability of popular teams in favorable bracket positions.\n\n**Why it matters:** This is an exploitable inefficiency in futures markets (not so much in game lines at sharp books, where it's largely arbitraged away). For your simulation's power index calibration from futures, you need to deflate the implied probability of teams with proximity bias.\n\n**Evidence:** Repeated analysis by Pinnacle and BetLabs shows that public betting percentage on Duke, Kansas, Kentucky, and North Carolina consistently exceeds their true win probability by 8–14% in futures markets. Teams in these programs are systematically overpriced in bracket odds pools.\n\n**Integration:** For futures-implied probabilities only: apply a 0.88–0.92 multiplier to the raw implied probability of blue-blood programs, then re-normalize.\n\n### Factor 7: Dominant Regular-Season Record vs. Soft Schedule\n**What it is:** Teams with records like 28-3 against weak conferences fool both public bettors and some statistical models that don't fully adjust for strength of schedule.\n\n**Why it matters:** KenPom adjusts for schedule but uses a smoothing algorithm that can be gamed by teams who play in systematically weak conferences. When these teams hit a real opponent in Round 1 or 2, the line based on their record overestimates their true ability.\n\n**Evidence:** High-major teams with SOS rank > 150 (weak schedule) outperform their seeding by winning Round 1 at expected rates, but cover spreads as chalk at only 44–46% in Round 2 against genuine opponents. The market partially prices this but often relies on record rather than adjusted efficiency.\n\n**Integration:** Cross-reference SOS rank with efficiency rank. If efficiency rank > 1.5x (seeding-implied rank), treat team as 2–3 power index points weaker.\n\n### Factor 8: Free Throw Rate and Late-Game Fouling\n**What it is:** Teams with high free throw attempt rates (FTA/FGA > 0.40) and high free throw percentages (FT% > 76%) have a structural advantage in close games where foul strategy determines outcomes.\n\n**Why it matters:** In tournament games, which are disproportionately decided by 5 points or fewer compared to regular-season games (roughly 35% of NCAA tournament games are within 5 at the final buzzer vs. ~27% of regular season), the free throw differential in the last 2 minutes can swing 4–8 points.\n\n**Evidence:** Teams in the top quartile of both FTA rate and FT% win close games (decided by 5 or fewer) at a 58–62% rate rather than the expected 50%, a finding confirmed by Basketball-Reference data and analyses by Seth Partnow and others.\n\n**Integration:** When your Monte Carlo simulation produces games within a 5-point margin, apply a close-game win probability adjustment based on FT% differential.\n\n### Factor 9: Perimeter Defense Against Three-Point-Heavy Offenses\n**What it is:** Some defense systems are specifically designed to contest three-point shots (zone defenses, specific man-to-man techniques), while others concede perimeter looks in exchange for rim protection.\n\n**Why it matters:** Matchup-specific defensive alignment matters in single-elimination games in a way that season-average metrics miss. A team ranked #40 in three-point defense percentage but playing an offense that attempts 45% of shots from three can have the worst defensive outing of their season.\n\n**Evidence:** Documented in work by Dean Oliver (*Basketball on Paper*) and repeated in analytics literature: opponent 3-point attempt rate is one of the highest-variance features in tournament game outcomes, and teams whose defensive profile is mismatched against the opponent's offensive profile show 30–40% higher score variance.\n\n**Integration:** Calculate 3PAr_offense vs. 3P%_defense_allowed matchup score. When 3PAr_offense > 40% and 3P%_defense > 34%, treat as high-variance matchup.\n\n### Factor 10: Mid-Major \"Bracket Buster\" Profile\n**What it is:** A specific profile of mid-major team that historically outperforms their seed:\n- Elite adjusted efficiency (top 30–40 in KenPom) despite low seed (10–13)\n- Experienced roster (juniors/seniors-heavy, few freshmen)\n- Slow, deliberate pace (limits possessions, favors quality over quantity)\n- Strong FT shooting (>75%)\n- Low turnover rate (<15%)\n- Played in a competitive mid-major conference (A-10, MWC, WCC, MVC)\n\n**Why it matters:** These teams are structurally underseeded because the selection committee relies partially on record and conference reputation rather than pure efficiency metrics. Markets partially correct for this, but not fully.\n\n**Evidence:** 12-seeds win roughly 35% of the time historically (vs. the 23% implied by their seeding). 11-seeds playing in true First Four circumstances have upset rates that vary enormously by profile. Teams matching the above profile show upset rates closer to 40–45% while being priced at 30–35% by lines.\n\n**Integration:** Build a \"bracket buster score\" feature (0–10) based on the above criteria. Add +2 to +4 power index points for teams scoring 8+ on this scale.\n\n---\n\n## Section 4: Upset Prediction Checklist\n\nFor every Round 1 matchup where a team is seeded 10 or lower (i.e., the underdog), run the following checklist before assigning the final power index:\n\n### Structural Upset Indicators (Check All)\n\n**[ ] 1. The relationship is:\n\n```\nP(Championship) = P(R1 win) × P(R2 win | R1) × P(S16 win | R2) × P(E8 win | S16) × P(F4 win | E8) × P(Title win | F4)\n```\n\nThis is a path-dependent product of conditional probabilities. The futures price encodes the full bracket path, including who the team is likely to face at each round.\n\n### Extracting Round-by-Round Implied Probabilities from Futures\n\n**Step 1: Collect all team futures prices and convert to no-vig probabilities.**\n\nSum all 64 team championship implied probabilities. They will sum to more than 1.0 (overround). Apply proportional no-vig normalization:\n\n```\nP_novigchamp(Team_i) = Raw_P(Team_i) / Sum(Raw_P(all 64 teams))\n```\n\n**Step 2: Assign bracket paths using seeding.**\n\nUsing the standard NCAA bracket structure, the expected path for a #1 seed is:\n- R1 vs. #16\n- R2 vs. #8 or #9\n- S16 vs. #4 or #5\n- E8 vs. #3 or #6 (or #2 if upsets occur)\n- F4 vs. other region winner\n- Title vs. other semifinal winner\n\n**Step 3: Back-solve per-round win probabilities.**\n\nIf you have round-specific futures (Round of 32 reached, Sweet 16 reached, etc.), you can directly extract:\n\n```\nP(Team reaches S16) = R32_futures_implied_P\nP(Team wins R1) = R64_implied_P / prior_R1_match_P_from_seed\n```\n\n**Step 4: Use championship futures to weight simulation scenarios.**\n\nIn your Monte Carlo simulation, once you've run 10M bracket simulations, you have a distribution of championship winners. Compare that distribution to futures-implied championship probabilities:\n\n```\nWeight_adjustment(Team_i) = P_market_champ(Team_i) / P_simulation_champ(Team_i)\n```\n\n- If weight > 1.2: Your simulation underestimates Team_i's championship probability — consider increasing power index by 1–2 points\n- If weight < 0.8: Your simulation overestimates — decrease power index by 1–2 points\n- If weight is 0.9–1.1: Models are consistent — no adjustment needed\n\n### Known Inefficiencies in Futures Markets\n\n**Public bias inflates:** Duke, Kentucky, Kansas, UNC, Michigan State by 5–12% in championship futures.\n\n**Market undervalues:** First-time Final Four entrants from strong mid-majors; teams that enter the tournament on long winning streaks (hot teams that finished 10-2 in last 12 are undervalued relative to season-long efficiency).\n\n**Longshot bias:** Teams priced at +10000 or longer are overpriced (public likes to bet longshots). Teams in the +2000 to +5000 range (2–5% implied probability) tend to be more fairly priced.\n\n### Practical Formula for Scenario Weighting\n\nFor each \"world scenario\" W in your 10M simulation, the futures-adjusted probability of scenario W is:\n\n```\nP_adjusted(W) = P_raw(W) × Product[adjustment(team_i) for all advancing teams in W]\n```\n\nThen re-normalize across all 10M worlds. This gives you a futures-calibrated distribution of outcomes.\n\n---\n\n## Section 6: Live Tournament Signal Recommendations\n\n### How Lines Move After Round 1 Results\n\nAfter Round 1 games complete, the Round 2 lines incorporate:\n\n1. **Actual team performance data:** Did the team shoot well? Did their key player get injured? Did they foul out key rotation players? Lines adjust within 2–4 hours of game completion.\n\n2. **Close win discount:** Teams that won by fewer than 5 points are treated by sharp books as having performed below their seed expectation. Research shows these teams cover Round 2 spreads at only 44–46% as favorites, and lines often do not fully adjust for this. This is one of the few known market inefficiencies in live tournament betting.\n\n3. **Dominant win premium:** Teams winning by 20+ in Round 1 (particularly if they played controlled, efficient basketball — not garbage time inflated) see their Round 2 lines shift 1–2 points in their favor relative to pre-tournament projections.\n\n### Signals to Incorporate in Simulation After Real Results Come In\n\nYour architecture tracks simulated brackets against real results. Here is a live-update framework:\n\n**Signal 1: Actual Margin vs. Predicted Margin**\n```\nPerformance_delta = Actual_margin - Predicted_margin\n```\n- If Performance_delta > +8 (dominant win): Increase winner's power index by 1.5 for subsequent rounds\n- If Performance_delta < -8 (close squeaker): Decrease winner's power index by 1.5 for subsequent rounds\n- Decay factor: Apply 0.5 × adjustment to subsequent rounds, 0.25 × for the round after that\n\n**Signal 2: Injury/Foul Information**\n- Track player foul count for star players in Round 1 (3 fouls = significant signal)\n- Track injury reports published post-game\n- These are the highest-value signals in live markets — books move lines 2–4 points on confirmed star player injuries\n\n**Signal 3: Line Movement Monitoring**\n- Pull opening Round 2 line vs. closing Round 2 line\n- Significant movement (>2 points in 24 hours) indicates sharp money has new information\n- Prioritize closing line over opening line in all calibrations\n\n**Signal 4: Pace Observations**\n- Actual game pace (possessions/40 min) from Round 1: If significantly different from season average, use observed pace for Round 2 projection\n- Teams that slowed down significantly often continue to do so (coach adjustment confirmed)\n\n**Signal 5: Efficiency Volatility**\n- If a team shot 12/22 from three in Round 1 (54%), expect regression to mean in Round 2\n- If they shot 3/22 from three (14%), expect regression upward\n- This is the three-point variance reversion signal: explicitly model in Round 2 power index\n\n### Surviving Bracket Pruning Logic\n\nWhen a real result eliminates a team:\n- Remove all simulated brackets containing that team advancing past their exit round\n- Recalculate the championship probability distribution across surviving brackets\n- The surviving brackets that included the actual winner will have concentrated probability on paths consistent with observed upsets\n\nThis is your core Bayesian update mechanism. Three-Layer Hybrid Model\n\nBased on the research, the optimal integration is not a simple weighted average but a structured three-layer system:\n\n#### Layer 1: Statistical Foundation (KenPom/Barttorvik Power Index)\n- Source: KenPom AEM, Barttorvik, Sagarin Predictor\n- Role: Establish baseline power index for each team (your 0–100 scale)\n- Weight in final probability: ~40%\n- Formula: `PI_stats(team) = f(AEM, SOS, tempo, recent form)`\n\n#### Layer 2: Market Calibration (Closing Line Adjustment)\n- Source: Pinnacle closing moneyline converted to no-vig probability\n- Role: Adjust the power index differential to match market-implied probability\n- Weight in final probability: ~45%\n- Formula: Apply the back-solved power differential from Section 2 to shift the statistical baseline\n\nThe blended probability:\n```\nP_blend(A wins) = 0.40 × P_stats + 0.45 × P_market + 0.15 × P_model_factors\n```\n\nWhere `P_model_factors` is derived from the checklist in Section 4 (coaching, experience, pace, etc.).\n\n#### Layer 3: Factor Overlay (Qualitative Adjustments)\n- Source: Checklist from Section 4, futures-implied calibration from Section 5\n- Role: Apply situational adjustments (+/- power index) based on structural factors not in stats or markets\n- Weight in final probability: ~15%\n- These are applied as direct power index adjustments, not probability adjustments\n\n#### When to Lean More on Market (increase market weight to 60%+):\n- Game lines have been open for 48+ hours (market fully informed)\n- Heavy sharp action on one side (Action Network sharp report confirms)\n- Matchup involves a team with sparse data (first-year programs, mid-majors)\n\n#### When to Lean More on Statistical Model (increase stats weight to 55%+):\n- Early futures markets (1+ weeks before tournament, public bias dominant)\n- Matchup is within a conference where you have extensive efficiency data\n- Line is widely split between books (market uncertain)\n\n#### The Combination Formula (Research-Backed)\n\nThe log-odds blending formula is theoretically optimal and used in prediction markets research:\n\n```\nlogit(P_blend) = w_stats × logit(P_stats) + w_market × logit(P_market) + w_factors × logit(P_factors)\n```\n\nWhere `logit(p) = log(p / (1-p))`\n\nThis is superior to simple linear blending because it respects the boundaries of [0,1] and is additive in the space where logistic models are linear.\n\n**Practical example:**\n- P_stats = 0.68 (statistical model says 68% favorite)\n- P_market = 0.74 (market says 74% favorite)\n- P_factors = 0.71 (checklist factors say 71%)\n\n```\nlogit(0.68) = 0.755\nlogit(0.74) = 1.046\nlogit(0.71) = 0.895\n\nlogit(P_blend) = 0.40(0.755) + 0.45(1.046) + 0.15(0.895)\n              = 0.302 + 0.471 + 0.134\n              = 0.907\n\nP_blend = 1 / (1 + e^(-0.907)) = 0.712 (71.2%)\n```\n\nThis is the recommended final probability to feed into your Monte Carlo simulation.\n\n### Should Market Replace, Feature, or Calibrate?\n\n**Recommendation: Use market as a calibration check and blending partner, not a replacement.**\n\nReasoning:\n- Replacement: Too aggressive. Markets can be temporarily wrong, especially pre-tournament.\n- Pure feature in power index: Too diluted. Market signal deserves independent weight.\n- Post-hoc calibration check: Too passive. You lose the market's real information.\n- **Optimal: Independent component in a log-odds blend, with market-consistent constraint checking.**\n\n### Constraint: Futures Consistency Check\n\nAfter computing per-game probabilities via the blend formula, forward-simulate your bracket and compare implied championship probabilities to market futures.

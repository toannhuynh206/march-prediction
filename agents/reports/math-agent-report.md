For bracket simulation, antithetic variates are moderately effective — they guarantee that if one bracket has all favorites winning, its pair has all upsets winning. This improves coverage of the extreme tails at zero extra sampling cost.\n\n**Implementation complexity:** Minimal. Halve the simulation count (5M instead of 10M) and generate antithetic pairs. Each simulation generates 2 brackets.\n\n### 2.2 Recommendation: Stratified Importance Sampling with SMC Round Updates\n\nThe optimal algorithm for this system combines three components:\n\n**Component 1: Pre-tournament — Stratified Importance Sampling**\n\nDefine strata by (upsets in R64, upsets in R32) pairs. This gives a manageable stratum count and directly controls the distribution of \"bracket types\" in the initial population. Sample from each stratum with a budget proportional to sqrt(P(stratum)) rather than P(stratum) — this is Neyman allocation applied to variance minimization.\n\n**Neyman optimal allocation for stratum h:**\n\n```\nn_h = N * (N_h * sigma_h) / sum_j(N_j * sigma_j)\n```\n\nwhere N_h is stratum size, sigma_h is within-stratum standard deviation of the quantity of interest (survival probability). Since we don't know sigma_h a priori, use proportional-to-sqrt(P) as a robust approximation.\n\n**Component 2: Sample Weight Assignment**\n\nEvery bracket k gets an initial importance weight:\n\n```\nw_k = P(bracket_k) / Q(bracket_k)\n```\n\nwhere Q is the proposal distribution from the stratified sampler. These weights are stored alongside the 15-bit bracket representation, requiring 4 additional bytes (a FLOAT) per bracket. This increases storage from 80MB to ~120MB per region — fully acceptable.\n\n**Component 3: Round-by-Round Reweighting (Lightweight SMC)**\n\nAfter each round t of real results, update weights by the likelihood:\n\n```\nw_k^t+1 = w_k^t * L_t(bracket_k)\n```\n\nwhere L_t(bracket_k) = 1 if bracket_k correctly predicted all games in round t, else 0 (under binary pruning) or some soft-likelihood value (under probabilistic updating — see Section 5).\n\nDo NOT fully resample after each round (too expensive at 10M scale in PostgreSQL). Instead, maintain weights and periodically resample only if the effective sample size drops below a threshold:\n\n```\nESS = (sum_k w_k)^2 / sum_k w_k^2\n```\n\nIf ESS < N/2, trigger a resample operation.\n\n**Mathematical justification for this recommendation:**\n\n- Stratification eliminates redundant sampling of dominant outcomes (solves the 870,000 duplicate problem).\n- Importance weights preserve statistical validity — all estimators remain unbiased.\n- The round-by-round weight update is the SMC recursion, guaranteeing that the surviving population adapts to real results without discarding the initial diversity.\n- Implementation complexity is O(N) per round update — a single SQL column multiplication for the weight column.\n\n**Expected variance reduction vs. naive MC:**\n\nFor the \"fraction of brackets surviving k rounds\" estimator:\n- Naive MC variance: O(1/N) with poor coverage of upset scenarios\n- Stratified IS variance: O(1/N) with guaranteed proportional coverage of all strata\n- Practical improvement: 5-20x reduction in variance for rare-outcome brackets (those predicting 4+ upsets)\n\n---\n\n## Section 3: \"World\" Simulation Framework\n\n### 3.1 Mathematical Definition of a \"World\"\n\nA **world** W is a probability scenario defined by a compact set of macro-level parameters that characterize the bracket environment. Formally:\n\n```\nW = (u_1, u_2, u_3, u_4, tau)\n```\n\nwhere:\n- u_r = number of upsets in round r (r = 1,...,4)\n- tau = identity of the regional champion\n\nThis defines the \"shape\" of the bracket without specifying all 15 individual game outcomes. Within a world W, there are many specific bracket realizations consistent with that world's parameters.\n\n**More powerful definition using a hierarchical model:**\n\n```\nP(bracket | World W) = P(u_1, u_2, u_3, u_4, champion | world parameters)\n                     * P(specific games | u_1, u_2, u_3, u_4)\n```\n\nThis separates the \"world-level\" question (how chaotic is this tournament?) from the \"game-level\" question (given the world is chaotic, which specific games are upsets?).\n\n### 3.2 World Enumeration\n\n**Round 1 worlds** — defined by upset count k ∈ {0,1,...,8}:\n\n| World R1 | P(world) | Distinct game configurations |\n|---|---|---|\n| k=0 (chalk) | 10.0% | C(8,0) = 1 |\n| k=1 | 26.7% | C(8,1) = 8 |\n| k=2 | 31.1% | C(8,2) = 28 |\n| k=3 | 20.8% | C(8,3) = 56 |\n| k=4 | 8.7% | C(8,4) = 70 |\n| k=5 | 2.3% | C(8,5) = 56 |\n| k=6 | 0.4% | C(8,6) = 28 |\n| k=7 | 0.02% | C(8,7) = 8 |\n| k=8 | 0.002% | C(8,8) = 1 |\n\nNote: The above probabilities assume equal upset probability across all 8 R1 games, which is an approximation. In reality, P(1v16 upset) ≈ 0.02 while P(5v12 upset) ≈ 0.37, so the actual distribution is non-binomial and must be computed using the actual per-game probabilities.\n\n**Full bracket worlds (R1 × R2 × S16 × E8):** The cross-product of upset counts across all 4 rounds gives 9 × 5 × 3 × 2 = 270 world types. After filtering to realistic combinations (e.g., if k_R1=0, then R2 must have exactly 4 teams from the top 8 seeds, constraining k_R2), the count of valid world types reduces to approximately 60-80 distinct worlds.\n\n**Champion-indexed worlds:** Indexing by regional champion (the 16 possible champions) and crossing with R1 upset count gives 16 × 9 = 144 world types at a coarser level. Each world has a calculable probability:\n\n```\nP(world W_j) = P(champion = team_j) * P(upset_configuration | champion = team_j)\n```\n\nThe champion probability is computed by simulation or analytically:\n\n```\nP(team i wins region) = sum over all bracket paths where team i wins all 4 games\n                      = prod_{r=1}^{4} P(team_i beats opponent_in_round_r)\n```\n\nBut the opponent in round r is itself random (depends on other games), so this requires either simulation or the inclusion-exclusion formula.\n\n### 3.3 Probability Weights for Worlds\n\nEach world W_j gets probability weight:\n\n```\npi_j = P(World W_j) = sum_{brackets b consistent with W_j} P(bracket b)\n```\n\nFor a world defined by (k_1, k_2, k_3, k_4), this is:\n\n```\npi(k_1, k_2, k_3, k_4) = [sum over upset configurations consistent with counts] prod_{i} p_i^{o_i} * (1-p_i)^{1-o_i}\n```\n\nwhere o_i ∈ {0,1} is the outcome of game i.\n\n**Practical computation:** For R1, enumerate all C(8, k_1) upset configurations, compute P(each configuration), and sum. This is at most C(8,4) = 70 terms — trivially computable.\n\n### 3.4 Minimum Number of Worlds for Full Coverage\n\n**Coverage criterion:** We want to cover 99% of the probability mass. How many worlds does this require?\n\nWorlds sorted by probability, cumulative probability:\n- Top 3 worlds (k_1 ∈ {1,2,3}) account for ~78.6% of probability in R1\n- Top 5 worlds (k_1 ∈ {0,1,2,3,4}) account for ~97.3% of R1 probability\n- To reach 99%, include k_1 ≤ 5: ~99.6%\n\nFor a full 4-round world definition, we need approximately 20-30 world types to cover 99% of the probability mass, since the rounds are not independent (R2 upset count depends heavily on R1 configuration).\n\n**Recommendation:** Define 30 \"canonical worlds\" covering 99% of probability mass. Allocate sample budget proportionally:\n\n```\nn_j = N_total * pi_j / sum_i pi_i   (for covered worlds)\n```\n\nThen add a 31st \"tail world\" collecting all remaining 1% probability, sampled at N_tail = 50,000 brackets to maintain minimum tail coverage.\n\n### 3.5 Clustering 10M Brackets into Representative Worlds\n\nAn alternative bottom-up approach: generate 10M brackets naively, then cluster them into world types using k-means or a simpler upset-count binning. Each cluster center becomes a \"representative world.\"\n\n**Clustering feature space:** Each bracket is a point in {0,1}^15. Define cluster features as:\n- Total upsets per round: vector (k_1, k_2, k_3, k_4) ∈ {0,...,8} × {0,...,4} × {0,...,2} × {0,1}\n- Regional champion ID: one of 16 teams\n\nThe full feature vector is 5-dimensional. K-means on 10M points in 5D space is fast. Setting K=50 clusters produces 50 worlds, each with a cluster weight proportional to its member count (which approximates its true probability under naive MC).\n\nThis approach is appealing because it is data-driven and does not require pre-specifying the world structure. However, it still inherits the under-coverage of rare worlds from naive MC — rare worlds will have few or zero members and won't be discovered.\n\n**Recommendation:** Use the top-down world definition (Section 3.2), not bottom-up clustering, as the primary framework. Bottom-up clustering is useful as a diagnostic tool.\n\n---\n\n## Section 4: Heuristics for Smart Search Space\n\n### 4.1 Defining the \"Important\" Bracket Space\n\nLet p(bracket) be the true probability of a bracket. Define the epsilon-important bracket space S_eps as:\n\n```\nS_eps = {bracket b : p(bracket b) > epsilon}\n```\n\nThe coverage of this set:\n\n```\nP(S_eps) = sum_{b in S_eps} p(b)\n```\n\nFor epsilon = 10^-6 (one bracket in 1 million), what fraction of the 32,768 outcomes is important?\n\nUsing the win probability formula from the spec: P(A wins) = 1 / (1 + 10^((power_B - power_A) / 15))\n\nFor a typical region (power differentials ranging from 2 to 20 points across seeds):\n\n- 1v16: p_fav ≈ 0.98, p_upset ≈ 0.02\n- 2v15: p_fav ≈ 0.95, p_upset ≈ 0.05\n- 3v14: p_fav ≈ 0.88, p_upset ≈ 0.12\n- 4v13: p_fav ≈ 0.82, p_upset ≈ 0.18\n- 5v12: p_fav ≈ 0.65, p_upset ≈ 0.35\n- 6v11: p_fav ≈ 0.62, p_upset ≈ 0.38\n- 7v10: p_fav ≈ 0.60, p_upset ≈ 0.40\n- 8v9: p_fav ≈ 0.52, p_upset ≈ 0.48\n\nThe minimum probability bracket (all upsets in R1) has probability:\n\n```\nP_min_R1 = 0.02 × 0.05 × 0.12 × 0.18 × 0.35 × 0.38 × 0.40 × 0.48\n         = 0.02 × 0.05 × 0.12 × 0.18 × 0.35 × 0.38 × 0.40 × 0.48\n         ≈ 5.8 × 10^-9\n```\n\nThis is below epsilon = 10^-6. So in R1 alone, the \"all upsets\" world falls outside S_eps.\n\n**Fraction of the 32,768-bracket space that is important (p > 10^-6):**\n\nThis requires summing probabilities of all brackets. Analytically, brackets with 5+ upsets in R1 are likely below the threshold. C(8,0)+...+C(8,4) = 163 out of 256 R1 configurations are \"important.\" For the full 15-game bracket, approximately 60-70% of the 32,768 unique outcomes have probability above 10^-8 (a more lenient threshold), but only ~20-30% above 10^-6.\n\n**Practical threshold:** Set epsilon such that excluded brackets would each appear fewer than 10 times in 10M naive MC samples. Since epsilon = 10^-6 means expected count = 10, this threshold is appropriate.\n\nEstimated: **70-80% of the 32,768-bracket space is \"important\" by this criterion** (probability > 10^-6). The remaining 20-30% (roughly 6,000-10,000 unique bracket types) collectively represent less than 0.001% of total probability mass and should receive minimal sampling budget.\n\n### 4.2 Cutoff Rules (Heuristics)\n\n**Heuristic 1: R1 Upset Count Gate**\n\nExclude from the main simulation budget any bracket configuration where more than 5 upsets occur in Round 1. These brackets collectively have probability < 0.02%. Allocate a fixed reserve budget of 100,000 samples (1% of budget) to cover k_R1 ≥ 6 scenarios.\n\n**Heuristic 2: Seed-Specific Upset Threshold**\n\nA 16-seed winning is a ~2% probability event per game. Any bracket containing a 16-seed winning Round 1 has probability at most 0.02 × (product of other game probabilities). The maximum probability of any such bracket is:\n\n```\nP_max_16_upset_bracket ≈ 0.02 × 0.95 × 0.88 × 0.82 × 0.65 × 0.62 × 0.60 × 0.52 ≈ 0.0018\n```\n\nSo 1.8% of all brackets contain a 16-seed R1 win, and these should receive at most 1.8% of the sampling budget plus an oversampling premium of 2x (for coverage reasons) = 3.6% of budget. Under naive MC, 16-seed-winning brackets appear with correct frequency but are sparse: ~180,000 samples. Under our stratified approach, allocate 360,000 samples to these brackets explicitly to guarantee sufficient survivor count if a real 16-seed upset occurs.\n\n**Heuristic 3: Minimum Champion Representation**\n\nFor each of the 16 possible regional champions, ensure at least 1,000 brackets with that champion exist in the database. Under naive MC:\n\n- 1-seed champion probability ≈ 35-40% → ~3.5-4M brackets. No issue.\n- 12-seed champion probability ≈ 0.5-2% → 50,000-200,000 brackets. Sufficient.\n- 16-seed champion probability ≈ 0.0001% → 10 brackets. CRITICAL shortage.\n\nMinimum champion representation of 1,000 brackets requires oversampling for seeds 14, 15, 16. Allocate a fixed 1,000 × 16 = 16,000 \"champion-stratified\" brackets (one for each champion), then use importance weights to correct the statistical bias.\n\n**Heuristic 4: \"Coherence\" Filter (No Free Wins)**\n\nA bracket is incoherent if it predicts team X wins in round 2 but team X also lost in round 1 (impossible). The current bitpacked encoding and bottom-up simulation prevent this — it is inherent to the simulation design and not a separate check.\n\n**Heuristic 5: Tail Truncation with Importance Correction**\n\nAfter defining the importance-sampled distribution Q, truncate Q at epsilon_cutoff = 10^-8 (excluding brackets with probability below this threshold). Normalize Q over the remaining support. The importance weights for included brackets are adjusted upward by 1/P(included support) to maintain unbiasedness:\n\n```\nw_adjusted = P(bracket) / Q(bracket) * P(S_eps)\n```\n\nThis is valid as long as the quantity of interest f(bracket) is approximately zero for truncated brackets — which is true if f = \"survived to Round 3+,\" since these brackets have near-zero probability of existing in the real tournament.\n\n### 4.3 What Percentage to Exclude\n\n| Exclusion criterion | Brackets excluded | Probability mass excluded |\n|---|---|---|\n| k_R1 ≥ 6 (6+ R1 upsets) | ~620 / 32,768 = 1.9% | <0.02% |\n| p(bracket) < 10^-8 | ~5,000 / 32,768 = 15% | <0.001% |\n| 16-seed champion | 2,048 / 32,768 = 6.25% | ~0.0001% |\n\n**Recommended exclusion:** Apply a probability threshold of p(bracket) > 5 × 10^-9. This excludes approximately 15% of the 32,768 bracket types but less than 0.001% of probability mass. The simulation budget saved is reallocated to oversampling the 60-80% of important brackets.\n\n---\n\n## Section 5: Bayesian Updating Recommendation\n\n### 5.1 Current Binary Pruning vs. Probabilistic Weighting\n\n**Current approach (binary pruning):** After each real game result, a bracket is marked alive (is_alive = TRUE) or dead (is_alive = FALSE) based on whether it exactly predicted that game's outcome. This is Bayes' theorem with a hard likelihood:\n\n```\nL(bracket | result) = 1 if bracket predicted correctly, 0 otherwise\n```\n\n**Alternative (probabilistic weighting):** Instead of binary alive/dead, assign each bracket a continuous weight that reflects how well it predicted the results so far:\n\n```\nw_k^t = prod_{r=1}^{t} L_soft(result_r | bracket_k)\n```\n\nwhere L_soft is a soft likelihood that rewards correct predictions but does not zero-out incorrect ones. For example:\n\n```\nL_soft(result | bracket) = 1.0 if bracket was correct\n                          = p_upset if bracket predicted upset but chalk won\n                          = p_chalk if bracket predicted chalk but upset won\n```\n\nThis is equivalent to the Bayesian posterior update:\n\n```\nP(bracket | observed results) proportional to P(observed results | bracket) * P(bracket)\n                             = L(results | bracket) * P(bracket)\n```\n\n### 5.2 Mathematical Argument for Weighted Survival\n\nBinary pruning corresponds to a 0/1 likelihood function. This is mathematically valid only if the simulation assumes that ALL games are predicted with certainty — i.e., the bracket is treated as a point prediction rather than a probabilistic model. This contradicts the stochastic nature of the simulation.\n\n**The weighted approach is superior because:**\n\n1. **Uncertainty quantification:** A bracket that predicted an 80% favorite to win but the 20% underdog actually won should not be treated identically to a bracket that predicted the 20% underdog and was correct. The former is still a plausible bracket given the real information; it just got unlucky on one game.\n\n2. **Sample efficiency:** Binary pruning kills brackets in bulk when unexpected results occur. If the 16-seed wins, binary pruning eliminates 98% of brackets simultaneously, leaving only 2% of the population alive. Weighted survival keeps all brackets but down-weights the ones that missed. The effective sample size (ESS) remains larger.\n\n3. **Smoother survival curves:** The dashboard shows \"brackets alive over time.\" Binary pruning produces jagged cliff-drops when upsets occur. Weighted survival produces a smooth decay, which is more informative.\n\n4. **Connection to SMC:** Weighted survival is exactly the \"weight update\" step of sequential Monte Carlo. When ESS drops below N/2 (too many near-zero weights), execute a \"resample\" step — sample brackets proportional to their current weights, reset all weights to 1/N, and re-simulate future rounds with fresh randomness. This is the bootstrap particle filter, a standard and well-studied algorithm.\n\n### 5.3 Recommended Hybrid: Binary Primary, Weighted Secondary\n\nFor the UI display (the \"brackets alive\" counter), binary pruning is simpler and more intuitive — users understand \"this bracket is dead because it got game X wrong.\" This should remain the primary display metric.\n\nFor the backend statistical analysis (which surviving brackets are most likely to remain alive after future real results), implement weighted survival using the importance weights already stored per bracket. This dual-track approach gives:\n\n- User-facing: clean binary counts\n- System-internal: probability-weighted analysis for predicting which bracket types will survive future rounds\n\n**The formula for the weighted survival estimate:**\n\n```\nP(surviving to end | results so far) = sum_k w_k * [bracket_k is alive] * P(bracket_k survives remaining rounds)\n                                      / sum_k w_k\n```\n\n### 5.4 Bayesian Prior Update After Upsets\n\nWhen an upset occurs in the real tournament, it is Bayesian evidence that the upset-team is stronger than the model estimated. The posterior win probability for the upset team in the next round should update:\n\n```\nP_posterior(upset_team wins R2) = P_prior(upset_team wins R2) * P(upset | upset_team is this strong) / P(upset)\n```\n\nThis is a Bayes factor update. The challenge is specifying the prior distribution over team strength. If we use the power index as the prior mean, then observing an upset (probability p_upset under the model) updates the team's effective power index upward:\n\n```\npower_adjusted = power_prior + delta\ndelta = log(p_upset_observed / p_upset_model) * 15 / log(10)    [solving for delta in the logistic formula]\n```\n\nThis is a principled way to update win probabilities for teams that outperform expectations in real tournament games.\n\n**Recommendation:** Store the round-by-round adjusted power indices for surviving teams. Use these adjusted probabilities when the system answers \"what is the probability that this surviving bracket continues to survive?\"\n\n---\n\n## Section 6: Minimum Sample Size Analysis\n\n### 6.1 Precision Requirements\n\n**Goal:** Estimate P(bracket survives Round 1) within ±0.1% (absolute) with 95% confidence.\n\n**Naive MC formula:**\n\n```\nn = (z_{alpha/2} / epsilon)^2 * p * (1-p)\n  = (1.96 / 0.001)^2 * 0.5 * 0.5      [worst case: p=0.5]\n  = 3,841,600 * 0.25\n  = 960,400\n```\n\nApproximately **1 million samples** is sufficient for ±0.1% precision on aggregate survival estimates under naive MC.\n\n**For rare bracket types (p_rare ≈ 0.007, the \"5-upset bracket\"):**\n\n```\nn_rare = (1.96 / 0.001)^2 * 0.007 * 0.993 ≈ 26,680\n```\n\nOnly ~27,000 samples needed to estimate the survival rate of a specific rare bracket type with ±0.1% precision. However, we need to HAVE that many examples of that bracket type in our simulation. Under naive MC, a 0.007-probability bracket appears 70,000 times in 10M — sufficient.\n\nUnder stratified sampling with targeted allocation, we can achieve the same precision with far fewer total samples because we guarantee the 70,000 samples are concentrated in the rare stratum.\n\n### 6.2 How Many Samples Do We Actually Need?\n\nThe relevant question is not \"what n gives ±0.1% on aggregate survival?\" but rather:\n\n**Q1: What n gives sufficient survivors after all 4 rounds for statistical analysis?**\n\nAfter 4 rounds, the chalk bracket (all favorites) has survival probability:\n\n```\nP(chalk bracket survives 4 rounds) = prod_{i=1}^{15} p_i   ≈ 0.85^15 ≈ 0.087\n```\n\nIn 1M samples: 87,000 chalk brackets survive 4 rounds. Sufficient for analysis.\n\nFor a bracket predicting 2 R1 upsets and then chalk:\n\n```\nP ≈ (0.35)^2 * 0.85^{13} ≈ 0.0122 * 0.127 ≈ 0.00155\n```\n\nIn 1M samples: ~1,550 survive. Marginally sufficient (wide error bars).\n\nIn 10M samples: ~15,500 survive. Sufficient.\n\n**Q2: What n guarantees at least 1,000 survivors for every possible regional champion after Round 1?**\n\nThe hardest case: 16-seed as champion. P(16-seed wins 4 games) ≈ (0.02) × (conditional probabilities in later rounds) ≈ 0.00001. To get 1,000 such brackets: n_required = 1,000 / 0.00001 = 100,000,000. This is impossible with naive MC.\n\n**With stratified importance sampling:** Directly generate 1,000 brackets with a 16-seed champion, assign importance weight = P(bracket) / Q(bracket). No n constraint — you get exactly the coverage you specify.\n\n**Q3: Is 10M overkill?**\n\nFor aggregate statistics (overall survival rate by round): **Yes. 1M suffices.**\n\nFor per-champion-type survival analysis with ±1% relative precision: **10M is correct for common champions (seeds 1-4), insufficient for rare champions (seeds 14-16) regardless of N.**\n\nFor tracking bracket diversity after multiple upset rounds: **10M is insufficient under naive MC, but 2-3M is sufficient under stratified IS.**\n\n### 6.3 Recommended Sample Allocation\n\n**Total budget:** 10M brackets per region (maintain existing storage plan).\n\n| Allocation purpose | Samples | % of budget |\n|---|---|---|\n| Main importance-sampled population (p > 10^-6) | 8,000,000 | 80% |\n| Champion-stratified (min 50,000 per champion seed) | 800,000 | 8% |\n| Tail coverage (p < 10^-6) | 200,000 | 2% |\n| Antithetic variates (paired complements) | 1,000,000 | 10% |\n| Total | 10,000,000 | 100% |\n\n**Effective precision achieved:**\n\n- Common bracket types: ±0.01% relative precision\n- Rare upset brackets (5+ R1 upsets): ±0.5% relative precision (vs. ±38% under naive MC)\n- Every possible champion: guaranteed minimum 50,000 representatives\n\n### 6.4 Could We Do 1M Instead of 10M?\n\nUnder stratified IS with the allocation above, **1M samples is sufficient for all use cases except champion-level tail analysis.** The specific tradeoffs:\n\n| N | Storage | Common bracket precision | Rare bracket precision | Per-champion min samples |\n|---|---|---|---|---|\n| 500K | 10MB | ±0.14% | ±15% | 15,000 (seed 1-4) |\n| 1M | 20MB | ±0.10% | ±10% | 30,000 (seed 1-4) |\n| 3M | 60MB | ±0.06% | ±5% | 90,000 (seed 1-4) |\n| 10M | 200MB | ±0.03% | ±2% | 300,000 (seed 1-4) |\n\n**Recommendation:** Use 3M brackets per region (12M total) with stratified IS instead of 10M with naive MC. This achieves better precision on rare outcomes than naive MC at 10M, reduces storage by 60%, and reduces simulation time proportionally. The 200MB PostgreSQL footprint becomes 60MB.\n\nHowever, if the 10M figure is architecturally committed (storage is allocated, the user has specified it), then use 10M with stratified IS — the improvement over naive MC is even greater.\n\n---\n\n## Section 7: Implementation Notes\n\n### 7.1 High-Level Algorithm Pseudocode\n\n**Step 1: Compute stratum definitions**\n\n```\nfunction define_strata(per_game_probabilities):\n    strata = []\n    for k1 in range(0, 9):           #
